I am Federico Camarda, an AD/ADAS Software Engineer based in Paris. Since 2017 my work has had a single focus: making vehicles smarter and safer — from prototype research to production code running on roads across Europe.

---

## From Italy to France

I grew up in Italy and studied Computer Engineering at the University of Genoa. In 2015 a double degree programme brought me to the Université de Technologie de Compiègne (UTC), a research-intensive university in northern France, where I enrolled in Complex Systems Engineering. It was a natural step towards autonomous vehicles: UTC hosts Heudiasyc, one of France's leading laboratories in autonomous driving research.

My Master's thesis at Heudiasyc gave me my first real taste of the field — not through papers, but behind the wheel of a prototype vehicle equipped with cameras, lidar and radar. Getting those sensors to agree on what the world looks like in real time, while the car is actually moving, is a different problem from anything you encounter in a lecture room. That experience set the direction for everything that followed.

## Four Years of PhD Research

After completing both Master's degrees in 2017, I joined Renault Group as a research engineer and PhD student, working in a joint programme between Renault and UTC's Heudiasyc laboratory. My research focused on road scene understanding for ADAS systems — specifically lane detection, HD map construction, and environment perception.

Four years of field tests, hundreds of hours of annotated driving data, and countless algorithm iterations led to a PhD thesis I defended in 2022. The title is dry in the way academic titles tend to be, but the underlying question was straightforward: how do you teach a vehicle to reliably understand the road it is driving on, at any speed, in any weather, in any country? We made progress. There is still a long way to go.

## An Unexpected Encounter in San Francisco

During a holiday trip to San Francisco I turned a corner and found myself standing next to a Zoox robotaxi prototype idling at a traffic light. No event badge, no planned visit — just the future sitting quietly in traffic alongside everyone else. I had to stop for a photo.

It is a small moment, but it captures something I find genuinely exciting about this field: the technology is out there, on public roads, improving every month. That is a rare thing in engineering.

## From Research to Production

After my PhD I transitioned into a full Software Engineer role at Ampère, Renault Group's electric vehicle software entity. The shift from research to production is substantial — algorithms now need to run reliably on constrained hardware, in vehicles being built by the thousands, validated against regulatory requirements across multiple markets.

I design, implement and validate perception algorithms that run in the ADAS stack of Renault vehicles today. The work is tangible in a way that research sometimes is not. Code I write ends up in cars on roads across Europe.

A milestone that meant a great deal to the whole team: the Renault 5 was named Car of the Year 2024. The ADAS software in that vehicle carries contributions from years of work by a team I am proud to be part of.
